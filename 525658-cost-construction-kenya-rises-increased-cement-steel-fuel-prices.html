Cost of Construction in Kenya Rises on Increased Cement, Steel and Fuel Prices

Kenyans will have to dig deeper into their pockets to build houses following the rise in costs of construction materials.

A new report by the Kenya National Bureau of Statistics (KNBS) showed that the cost of construction increased by 2.71% during the third quarter of the year.

The survey revealed almost all construction material indices increased except for paints, sanitary fittings, water fittings, water waste appliances and electrical fittings.

This saw the construction cost index increase from 113.38 in September 2022 to 116.45 a year later.

"The index of cement, steel, and reinforcement bars went up by 0.5% and 1.6%, respectively, from the second quarter to the third quarter of 2023. The index of hardcore, dense bitumen macadam, metal doors and windows all went up by 1.6%. Meanwhile, the transport and fuels index increased by 3% within the same period under consideration," KNBS stated.

What's the cost of building a 2-bedroom house?

Kenyan contractor Anthony Muriithi explained that the cost of building a permanent two-bedroom house varies depending on the materials used and location.

Muriithi, who deals with commercial and residential projects, revealed cost ranges from KSh 1.5 million to KSh 4 million in Nairobi.

"The cost of constructing a simple two-bedroom permanent house can vary depending on several factors, including location, quality of materials used, total area coverage, and timeline of construction. For example, in Nairobi county, a two-bedroom house ranges from KSh 1.5 million to KSh 4 million," he told TUKO.co.ke.

Why did steel prices rise?

Steel prices increased globally in three months following a market shortage of the steel supply.

Leading global market suppliers like China (52%) reported a slump in crude steel production, resulting in high prices.

Market indicators showed a 29.5% rise in steel prices from $1458.37 (KSh 181,057) per tonne reported in October 2022 to $1964.12 (KSh 243,845) in January 2023.

The rise in prices leading to increased construction cost could be transferred to the end users.
