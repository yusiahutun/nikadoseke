Rogue florists are hit by 1,600 complaints after taking customers' cash then failing to deliver bouq

June 04, 2024
blog

Two florists have been slammed for allegedly running fraud schemes - as the Better Business Bureau of Metro New York has warned customers about shopping with them. 

Roger Florist and Baby Blue Florist have racked up more than 1,600 complaints from customers who say they were scammed, according to the BBB's latest investigation. 

Customers say that they place an order with their credit card, and then an email arrives the same day informing them that that their order was cancelled. They're told they will be getting a refund, but the refund never comes.

Edward Williams, 53, a disabled retiree from Baltimore, who lives on a fixed income told DailyMail.com that he ordered flowers from Baby Blue Florists and was duped twice. 

The first order was for a sick friend who was in hospital, and the second to say thank you to hospital staff for their help. 

It was only after he relentlessly attempted to get his money back by getting his bank get involved and filing a complaint with the BBB that the business issued a refund.

'Why is this florist still in business? They are a fraud,' Williams said. 'I was fortunate enough to get my money but there are probably thousands who lost money.'

He is now warning others of the scam and telling buyers to 'be aware.'

Roger Florist claims to have a Melville, Long Island address, while Baby Blue Florist says they operate out of Oyster Bay in Long Island.

When individuals visit the Roger Florist an error appears on the page indicating that the website may be down. Though the BBB said they continue to process complaints and reviews about the company when they receive them.

On the Baby Blue Florist website there are numerous photos of floral arrangements for different occassions, including gift baskets and wreaths. The company lists many items on 'sale' and claim they specialize in 'last minute orders.'

The site also states 'the business has more than 50 years combined experience and that their member network allows them to service its customers nationwide in over 10,000 cities.'

A logo from the Better Business Bureau and the Society of American Florists appear on their site, but according to both organization their logos should not be displayed. 

'The firm is not currently a BBB Accredited Business with the Better Business Bureau serving metropolitan New York, the service area in which it is headquartered,' the agency told DailyMail.com.

On May 8, the BBB requested that Baby Blue Florist cease and desist all unauthorized used of the their trademarks, but they had not responded or complied with any of their requests, the agency said.

On Thursday, the American Society of Flowers (SAF) told DailyMail.com that Baby Blue Florists and Roger Florist are not members of their organization and never have been. 

'They are both using our name and logo illegally on their websites,' said SAF's CEO Kate Penn.

Penn said the American Society of Flowers is a national trade association that represents all segments of the U.S. floral industry with more than 2,500 member organizations.

She said the organization's law firm sent Roger Florist a cease-and-desist letter in May and will be doing the same for Baby Blue Flowers.

Furious customers not only voiced their complaints to the BBB but to also a review platform called Trust Pilot and ScamWatcher. 

A customer from Princeton, New Jersey, who identified themselves as 'Roger Florist Scam Victim,' reported their horrid experience in September 2022 and posted the scathing complaint with the caption: 'Cancelled my order but kept my money.'

The person shared that they were ordering flowers for a funeral and found Roger Florist after doing a Google search on florists in their area. 

After they placed the order and the credit charge went through the patron received an email an hour later informing them that the order was cancelled. 

The company claimed the flowers could not be delivered because there was no delivery service in that area, but was guaranteed a refund in 10 to 20 days. The complainant said the refund never came.

In the post, the person showed two different addresses that they allege are 'fake.' They noted that the Melville Long Island address is a Bank of America and the New Jersey address is a drug store. 

'They are scammers and have been doing this for a while under different names. They need to be shut down,' the person wrote, in part.

Earlier this month, another complaint was posted to Trust Pilot from a customer, who described herself as a 'senior on a fixed income.'

She said she placed a flower order on September 6 but was told her order had been cancelled after she made the payment. 

As of September 12, she said she was still waiting for the refund that the company promised to issue. 

'I truly cannot afford to lose any money through scams, she quipped. 'You should be ashamed!'

Claire Rosenzweig, president and CEO of the Better Business Bureau of Metro New York told DailyMail.com that 'consumers who have filed complaints with BBB on these two florists have reported similar practices of taking orders, then canceling them, followed by problems getting a refund.'

Though, she added that some customers did eventually get their money back after they filed a complaint with BBB they continue to urge customers to exercise caution.

'We urge anyone who has lost money to an online florist, or any other company, to file a complaint at BBB.org,' she said.  'We will do our best to resolve the issue as possible.'

BBB provides the following tips for purchasing flowers online:

Do your research. Go to BBB.org for BBB Business Profiles on more than 20,000 florists. Read what previous customers say, and see how the business responds to complaints. This information may help narrow down the list of online and local florists.

Know when to place your order. During the holidays, orders for floral arrangement increase dramatically, and prices often go up. Order early to ensure your order arrives on time.

Be a savvy shopper. When shopping online, take your time and read the fine print before submitting your order. Look for the return policy and be sure you understand the delivery process. Look for added fees like shipping, handling, and delivery. Review your order and verify the total before confirming your purchase. Find out how the business handles customer complaints and what recourse you will have if the floral arrangement is unsatisfactory.

Pay with a credit card. Credit cards provide additional protections, and it's easier to dispute charges. Debit, prepaid, or gift cards don't have the same protections as credit cards.

Know the delivery method. Some online florists have different delivery methods: same-day, international, standard, and guaranteed. Be sure you know which delivery service you are paying for and that you have allowed enough time. Some florists may offer a discount if you send your flowers to arrive a few days early since that helps them with the rush on holiday.

Make sure the business has your contact information. When it comes to flower delivery, there are times when a driver will need additional information or directions. Ensure the florist or retailer has a callback phone number to help them reach you and ensure your loved one gets what you expect.

Read the description. Online photos use a perfect setting and professional lighting to make the products look as attractive. Your final product may not look quite the same. Make sure you know what you're getting.

Don't be deceived by promotions. You may encounter online promotions during especially popular times like Mother's Day or Valentine's Day. Most have terms and conditions, so check that you're getting the savings you expect.

Track your purchases. When using a third party to deliver flowers, it's hard to tell when and if they were delivered. Sign up for confirmation emails to prove the order was placed and an alert when the order is delivered.

Take a photo. Ask the recipient to send you a photo of the delivered arrangement so that you can compare it to the online photo or what was promised in the shop. If the product differs considerably, you may want to ask for an adjustment in the price.

Source: BBB.org
