Drama student who stabbed two classmates during a film project found NOT guilty by insanity

August 08, 2024

A former drama student accused of stabbing two of his fellow students with a knife while filming a movie in March was found not guilty of assault charges on Friday after a judge found he was suffering from a psychotic episode at the time.

Jake Wascher, 21, of San Diego, is now facing years of state enforced psychiatric rehabilitation inside Whiting Forensic Hospital, a maximum-security institute for people acquitted of crimes on the basis of mental disease.

The ruling was made by Judge James T. Graham after a daylong hearing last week, following testimony from a psychiatrist who presented evidence to support the defense’s claims Wascher suffered a ‘brief psychotic disorder’ during the March 31 attack.

Graham met with Wascher five times and reviewed various medical documents prior to the hearing, leading him to concur the student had been ‘in the midst of a psychotic episode and suffered from paranoid delusions’.

The University of Hartford student's delusions included believing his victims were out to harm him, the world was about to end, and that he was being watched so as not to tell anyone.

Thomas Nee, 19, of South Windsor, suffered the most serious injuries in Wascher’s attack, suffering stabs wounds to the chest and back, leaving him in a critical condition.

Brandon Gasparino, 21, of New Fairfield, also suffered three stab wounds to his back and one to his chest.

Wascher attacked the two students in Gasparino’s on-campus apartment during a rehearsal for a class assignment, in which Wascher was an actor.

The two victims were setting up a shot in a bedroom inside the apartment when the attack occurred.

‘As we were setting up the cameras ... Jake Wascher came in with the knife and stabbed me and Thomas,’ Gasparino testified Friday, the Hartford Courant reported. ‘I assumed it was a joke because it didn’t make any sense I was getting stabbed.

Gasparino said he ended up on the floor, on his back, trying to defend himself from Wascher and screaming for him to ‘get away from me’.

Another resident of the apartment walked in and Wascher fled into the woods several hundred yards southeast of the campus.

He was apprehended two hours later after a policeman spotted him hiding between trees.

Both of the men testified they were shocked by the attack, insisting no conflict had occurred prior and the pair had never experienced trouble with Wascher before. He also said nothing during the attack, both of the men said.

‘He seemed a little bit spacey, but nothing out of the ordinary,’ Gasparino told the court.

Witness Will Moser, 19, agreed that Wascher seemed ‘spaced out’ and ‘wasn’t acting his normal self’, pacing back and forth before striking.

Dr. John Bonetti of the Institute of Living testified in the days prior to the attack that Wascher showed signs of illness and paranoid thoughts.

Bonetti said the defendant believed the end of the world was approaching and was concerned about his safety. He also believe he could determine someone’s intention to harm him by the size of their pupils and their diet choices.

On the day of the attack, he believed his two victims were about to kill him and heard a song that told him to kill them first.

Following Friday’s hearing, Wascher was committed to the Department of Mental Health and Addiction Services (DMHAS) so staff staff could evaluate his current mental health conviction.

In the months after the attack, Wascher’s condition returned to normal when he was medicated, officials said, but after missing a dose of anti-psychotic medication in jail he wrote a suicide note, jumped from the second-floor tier and then attacked a correction officer.

In a hearing scheduled for December 23, Judge Graham will review the findings of the DMHAS and then likely commit Wascher to the custody of the state Psychiatric Security Review Board, which will monitor Wascher’s treatment and determine when he should be released from custody.
